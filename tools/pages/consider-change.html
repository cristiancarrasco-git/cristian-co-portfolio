01 / The series

Five projects, one campaign spine

One voice across all five: a hard stat leads, plain language explains, Nature.org closes — the same hierarchy from a four-inch fold to a freeway underpass. One visual system: TNC green, halftone photo collage, heavy grotesque type. And escalating dimensionality: 2D map → poster → folded object → 3D space → motion.

02 / Project 1

Systems in Nature

Before any poster, a full taxonomy: Kingdom Animalia, organic cycles, the four spheres, ecophysiology, adaptation. Type size ranks concepts, hue groups systems, and hand-drawn branch lines tie 100+ terms into one readable network — the campaign's knowledge base. Adaptation, energy transfer, and human pressure become the stats every later project spends.

03 / Project 2

Three stats, three ecosystems

64%
OF NORTH AMERICAN SPECIES HIGHLY VULNERABLE TO EXTINCTION
20%
OF NA FORESTS PERMANENTLY CLEARED SINCE 2001
150B
GALLONS OF WATER USED DAILY IN THERMOELECTRIC PLANTS

Printed at 22×28 inches and built for the street: white grotesque on saturated fields survives glare, motion, and distance — the stat reads before the bus leaves. "Consider Change" is the hinge on every poster, color codes the crisis (orange / green / blue), and every number carries its source in the footer.

04 / Project 3

The Cost of Data Structure

An accordion that argues in folds. The outside pairs four tech-hub cities with their local stakes — San Francisco, Dallas, D.C., New York, each landmark in halftone collage. The inside opens into the payoff: a U.S. data-center grid with MW-scaled chips, hub markers, and a 44/44/11 energy split — 4.4% of national electricity. The reader controls the reveal; the fold is the narrative.

05 / Project 4

A message the freeway can't ignore

Site: the underpass at Emma Prusch Farm Park, San Jose — highlighting underconsidered infrastructure in underconsidered neighborhoods.

  • Create space for playRespect the relationship between everyday life, nature, and technology.
  • Address the local communityClaim Eastside San Jose as part of Silicon Valley's tech bubble — not outside it.
  • Sustainability firstThe installation is non-invasive to the local environment by design.
Takeaway

Same message. Every scale.

From a hand-drawn taxonomy to a freeway underpass, the campaign proves a system: lead with the number, ground it in place, close with Nature.org — and let each medium do what only it can.
